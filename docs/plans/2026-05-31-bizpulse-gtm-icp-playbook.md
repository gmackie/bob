# BizPulse GTM ICP Playbook

Date: 2026-05-31
Status: Draft v1 for GMA-17 discovery

## Summary

BizPulse should start with narrow, high-signal founder-led B2B teams that already run multiple active growth or product bets but do not yet have reliable operating cadence around them. The beachhead is not "all startups"; it is small teams where the cost of dropped follow-up, scattered customer evidence, and unclear bet ownership is already visible, but the team is still too small for heavyweight CRM, BI, or planning operations.

## Segment Tiers

### Tier A: Primary Beachhead

Founder-led B2B SaaS or AI workflow startups between 3 and 20 people.

Triggers:

1. Team size: 3 to 20 full-time contributors, with at least one founder still directly owning sales, product, or customer discovery.
2. Active bets: 5 to 20 concurrent GTM, onboarding, retention, pricing, or product-learning bets.
3. Toolchain: Slack or Discord, Linear or GitHub Issues, Notion or Google Docs, HubSpot or Airtable used lightly, spreadsheets for pipeline or experiment tracking.
4. Distribution: founder-led outbound, warm intros, small waitlist, App Store or web acquisition, or early design partners.
5. Buying signal: founder says they "lose the thread" after calls, experiments, or launches, or manually rebuilds weekly status from scattered notes.

Job to be done:

When the team is juggling customer conversations, launch work, and early revenue experiments, help the founder turn scattered signals into a prioritized operating view so they can decide what to follow up on, what to kill, and what to push next without adding operational drag.

Pain points:

1. Customer notes, product tasks, and sales follow-ups live in separate tools.
2. Active bets lack explicit owners, hypotheses, next actions, and kill criteria.
3. Weekly planning depends on founder memory instead of a shared system.
4. CRM feels premature, but spreadsheets and docs do not create momentum.
5. Mobile capture matters because many updates happen between calls, meetings, and travel.

Alternative tools:

1. Notion databases for manual operating docs.
2. Airtable bases for experiment or pipeline tracking.
3. HubSpot free CRM for contact and deal tracking.
4. Linear, Jira, or GitHub Issues for execution tasks.
5. Google Sheets for ad hoc bet, pipeline, and KPI logs.
6. Slack saved messages, reminders, and channel posts for informal follow-up.

Why this tier:

Tier A has acute pain, short buying paths, and enough operational complexity to value structure without requiring enterprise integrations. It is also reachable through founder communities, accelerators, indie SaaS networks, AI product communities, and Gmacko's existing distribution surface.

### Tier B: Stretch Segments

#### B1: Seed-stage Revenue Teams

B2B startups between 20 and 60 people with 2 to 8 GTM contributors and founder involvement still present.

Triggers:

1. Team size: 20 to 60 total, with a small sales, customer success, or growth function.
2. Active bets: 10 to 30 concurrent pipeline, activation, retention, lifecycle, or segment tests.
3. Toolchain: HubSpot or Salesforce, Linear or Jira, Notion or Confluence, Slack, customer call recording, BI dashboards.
4. Buying signal: head of growth, sales, or product operations wants a lighter way to connect GTM experiments to product and customer signals.

Job to be done:

When a small revenue team is trying to prove repeatability, help them connect customer evidence, pipeline movement, and experiment status so leadership can see which motions deserve more investment.

Pain points:

1. CRM captures activity but not the reasoning behind growth bets.
2. Product and GTM teams disagree about which customer signals matter.
3. Experiment reviews become slide updates instead of living operating context.
4. Existing dashboards show lagging metrics but not the decisions behind them.

Alternative tools:

1. HubSpot or Salesforce reports.
2. Productboard, Dovetail, or Gong for customer evidence.
3. Airtable, Notion, or Coda for experiment operating systems.
4. Amplitude, Mixpanel, Looker, or Metabase for metrics.
5. Asana, Jira, or Linear for task execution.

#### B2: Productized Services And Agencies

Small agencies, studios, or implementation consultancies between 5 and 30 people that run repeated client acquisition and delivery experiments.

Triggers:

1. Team size: 5 to 30 contributors.
2. Active bets: 5 to 15 concurrent outbound, partnership, offer, pricing, or delivery-process bets.
3. Toolchain: HubSpot, Pipedrive, Airtable, Notion, ClickUp, Slack, Google Workspace.
4. Buying signal: owner wants visibility into which offers, verticals, or acquisition channels are working without building a custom dashboard.

Job to be done:

When the agency is testing offers and delivery motions across multiple prospects or clients, help the owner see which bets are producing qualified conversations and profitable work.

Pain points:

1. Sales pipeline, delivery notes, and offer experiments are disconnected.
2. Follow-ups rely on owner discipline and are easy to miss.
3. CRM tracks contacts but not service-package learning.
4. Teams lack a simple ritual for deciding which niche, offer, or channel to double down on.

Alternative tools:

1. Pipedrive, HubSpot, or Folk for pipeline.
2. Airtable or Notion for client and experiment tracking.
3. ClickUp, Monday, or Asana for delivery work.
4. Google Sheets for revenue and channel analysis.

### Tier C: Deprioritized

#### C1: Solo Founders With Fewer Than 5 Active Bets

Reasons to deprioritize:

1. Pain may be real but is often solved by a notes app, spreadsheet, or personal task manager.
2. Lower willingness to pay until there is team coordination or revenue pressure.
3. Feedback can overfit toward individual productivity instead of team operating cadence.

Useful learning only if:

1. They have frequent customer calls.
2. They are already paying for CRM or analytics.
3. They use mobile heavily for capture and follow-up.

#### C2: Mature Sales Organizations Above 100 People

Reasons to deprioritize:

1. Buying path is slower and integration requirements are heavier.
2. Existing CRM, RevOps, BI, and enablement workflows are entrenched.
3. Needs will skew toward enterprise governance, permissions, and reporting before the core product is validated.

Useful learning only if:

1. A team lead wants a lightweight pilot outside the official CRM workflow.
2. The use case is discovery research or new-market incubation rather than core sales operations.

#### C3: Consumer Creators And Lifestyle Businesses

Reasons to deprioritize:

1. Bet tracking language may map poorly to their daily workflows.
2. Tool alternatives are cheap and simple.
3. Reachable volume can be high, but learning may pull BizPulse away from B2B operating problems.

Useful learning only if:

1. They run repeatable sponsorship, product launch, or paid community experiments.
2. They already manage collaborators and a structured pipeline.

## Sizing Estimate Methodology

Use a directional, assumption-driven sizing model until primary discovery produces stronger conversion and willingness-to-pay data.

### TAM Proxy

1. Define the universe as small B2B software, AI workflow, productized service, and startup teams in English-speaking markets.
2. Use public proxies: business registry counts, startup databases, accelerator cohorts, SaaS directories, LinkedIn company counts, CRM category reports, App Store business app category signals, and community membership counts.
3. Filter to teams with 3 to 60 people because BizPulse needs enough coordination pain without enterprise buying friction.
4. Apply a workflow-readiness filter for teams already using at least two of: CRM, issue tracker, team docs, Slack or Discord, analytics, call recording, Airtable or Notion database.
5. Apply an urgency filter for teams with at least 5 active customer, growth, or product bets in the current month.

Formula:

```text
Directional TAM accounts =
  small-team company universe
  x B2B or productized-service relevance rate
  x 3-to-60-person team-size rate
  x workflow-readiness rate
  x active-bet urgency rate
```

Revenue TAM proxy:

```text
Directional annual revenue TAM =
  directional TAM accounts
  x assumed annual contract value
```

Initial ACV assumptions should be scenario-based rather than fixed:

1. Low: founder self-serve or light team plan.
2. Base: small-team operating plan.
3. High: revenue or product team plan with more seats and integrations.

### Reachable Beachhead

Start from channels Gmacko can reach without paid enterprise demand generation.

Beachhead account sources:

1. Existing Gmacko users and App Store visitors who match startup, builder, or team-planning behavior.
2. Founder-led SaaS and AI startup communities.
3. Accelerator, studio, and operator networks.
4. GitHub, Product Hunt, LinkedIn, and founder newsletter audiences.
5. Warm intros from design partners, investors, and agency operators.

Beachhead model:

```text
Reachable beachhead accounts =
  owned and warm-network reachable audience
  x ICP-fit rate
  x problem-aware rate
  x interview or trial conversion rate
```

Discovery target for the first 60 days:

1. Build a named list of 150 to 250 Tier A accounts.
2. Contact 75 to 100 accounts through warm intros, founder communities, and direct founder outreach.
3. Book 15 discovery calls.
4. Convert 5 to 7 qualified teams into prototype or concierge pilots.
5. Use pilot activation, repeated weekly use, and willingness to pay as stronger sizing inputs than top-down category reports.

## JTBD Hypotheses To Validate

1. Founders do not need another CRM; they need a lightweight operating layer for active bets and follow-up.
2. The painful unit is not a task or a contact; it is a bet with evidence, owner, next action, and decision status.
3. Mobile capture is a meaningful differentiator when updates happen outside a desktop planning ritual.
4. Teams will adopt BizPulse faster if it complements current tools instead of replacing CRM, issue tracking, or docs on day one.
5. A weekly review workflow is the strongest retention loop because it turns captured signals into decisions.

## Interview Plan

Target 15 discovery calls across tiers:

1. Tier A founder-led B2B SaaS or AI workflow startups: 9 calls.
2. Tier B1 seed-stage revenue or growth teams: 3 calls.
3. Tier B2 productized service or agency operators: 2 calls.
4. Tier C edge-case solo founder with unusually high customer volume: 1 call.

Screening criteria:

1. Team has at least 3 people, except the single Tier C edge case.
2. Team has at least 5 active GTM, product, customer, or revenue bets.
3. Team uses at least 3 tools across communication, tasks, docs, CRM or pipeline, and metrics.
4. Respondent can describe a recent missed follow-up, unclear bet, stale experiment, or planning breakdown.

### Script

Opening:

1. "What does your team sell, who buys it, and what stage is the business in?"
2. "What are the most important growth, product, or customer-learning bets active right now?"
3. "Who owns deciding whether those bets continue, change, or stop?"

Current workflow:

1. "Walk me through what happens after a customer call, launch, outbound push, or experiment update."
2. "Where do notes, next actions, and evidence get captured?"
3. "Which tools are involved between the first signal and the next team decision?"
4. "How do you review active bets each week?"

Pain validation:

1. "Tell me about the last time an important follow-up or insight got lost."
2. "What breaks when you have too many active experiments or customer threads?"
3. "Where do you rely on memory, manual status updates, or spreadsheet cleanup?"
4. "Which parts of the workflow feel too lightweight, and which tools feel too heavy?"

Alternative tools and switching:

1. "What have you tried to solve this with?"
2. "What works well enough today?"
3. "Where do CRM, project management, docs, or dashboards fail to answer the real question?"
4. "What would need to be true for you to add a new tool to this workflow?"

Mobile and distribution:

1. "When do important updates happen away from your desk?"
2. "Do you use mobile today for capturing customer, sales, or product follow-up?"
3. "Would App Store availability make this easier to try, or would it not matter?"

Value and willingness to pay:

1. "If this problem disappeared, what would change in your weekly operating rhythm?"
2. "What would be the clearest sign that a tool was saving you time or improving decisions?"
3. "Who would need to approve paying for it?"
4. "What would you compare it against when deciding whether to pay?"

Close:

1. "Can we review one real active bet from your current workflow?"
2. "Who else on your team feels this pain most directly?"
3. "Would you be open to a lightweight prototype or concierge pilot?"

### Call Outputs

For each call, capture:

1. Segment and trigger match.
2. Number and types of active bets.
3. Current toolchain.
4. Strongest JTBD in the respondent's own words.
5. Top 3 pain points.
6. Alternative tools and why they are insufficient.
7. Buying owner and approval path.
8. Pilot fit: yes, maybe, or no.
9. Follow-up action and date.

## Qualification Rubric

Score each account from 0 to 2 on each dimension:

1. Active-bet volume: fewer than 5, 5 to 10, more than 10.
2. Tool fragmentation: fewer than 3 tools, 3 to 4 tools, more than 4 tools.
3. Decision urgency: nice-to-have, recurring annoyance, weekly executive or founder pain.
4. Mobile capture relevance: none, occasional, frequent.
5. Willingness to pay: unclear, possible, explicit budget or current paid alternative.

Prioritize accounts scoring 7 or higher for pilots. Use accounts scoring 5 to 6 for discovery only. Deprioritize accounts below 5 unless they reveal a new high-value segment pattern.

## Next Steps

2. Tag each account by team size, active-bet evidence, toolchain evidence, and warm-intro path.
3. Run 15 calls using the script above.
4. Select 5 to 7 pilot candidates based on rubric score and urgency.
5. Revisit segment tiers after the first 15 calls with evidence from call notes, not category assumptions.
